What type of t-test will you use?
Dependent t-tests generally require fewer participants than independent t-tests, because individual differences are controlled in the design by using the same participants (measured twice) or matched pairs.
Independent t-tests need to statistically account for the added “noise” introduced by individual differences between subjects.
Less between-subjects “noise” → smaller sample needed
Compares two separate groups (e.g., older versus younger adults).
Compares one group measured twice (e.g., pre/post) or matched pairs (e.g., parent–child).
Will the two groups being compared be about the same size?
Aim to recruit balanced groups to maximize the efficiency of your total sample.
When groups are unequal, statistical power is limited by the smaller group’s size–power gains flatten quickly when one group is much larger than the other.
e.g., a test comparing two groups of 10 (N = 20) is nearly as statistically powerful as a test comparing 10 vs. 100 (N = 110)
Promote group balance through stratified recruitment or quotas. For pre-post tests, use study designs that discourage participant dropout after the pre-test.
It’s not always possible to recruit equal groups; some variation in group size is common. Focus recruitment on the group you anticipate being smaller to improve power.
Will you use the standard two-tailed t-test?
When uncertain, choose a two-tailed test. It’s slightly less power-efficient but more flexible and widely accepted.
Two-tailed = tests for any difference between groups (conditions, measurements, paired subjects, etc.) in either direction (e.g., can test if A > B and if A < B).

Works for directional and non-directional hypotheses. Widely interpretable and accepted in peer review. Won’t “miss” a result if it’s opposite of the hypothesized direction.
Slightly larger required sample than one-tailed since it “spends” power testing differences in both directions.
One-tailed = tests a difference between groups (conditions, measurements, paired subjects, etc.) in one direction only (e.g., only tests if A > B, won’t test if A < B)
More power-efficient for tightly controlled research with well-justified directional hypotheses; smaller sample required compared to an equivalent two-tailed test.
Only for directional hypotheses–ignores effects in the opposite direction. Less accepted in peer review unless well justified a priori.

Alpha (p-value):



This is the p-value used to define result significance. Common choices: .05, .01, .001.
Larger p-values = fewer participants
>.05 is rarely acceptable, but <.01 may mean you’re collecting data from participants that you don’t need, unnecessarily risking their privacy
Minimum Detectable Effect Size
(Cohen's d):


T-test effect sizes: small (.2), medium (.5), large (.8). Many studies aim for between .2 and .5
Smaller effects = exponentially more participants
>.8 means you’ll likely collect data you can’t use, risking participant privacy with no scientific gain
Power (Beta - 1):
   


A power of .80 (80%) is standard.
Higher power = more participants
<.80 risks missing true effects and is rarely acceptable; >.90 may mean unnecessary data collection from participants
